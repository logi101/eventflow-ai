# Hebrew Language Rules and Guidelines

## Overview
Comprehensive guide for editing Hebrew text, covering spelling, punctuation, nikud (vocalization), common errors, and typography specific to Hebrew.

## Hebrew Alphabet and Spelling

### Basic Letters (אותיות)
- 22 letters in the Hebrew alphabet
- 5 final forms (sofit): ך, ם, ן, ף, ץ
- No uppercase/lowercase distinction

### Vowel Pointing (Nikud/נִקּוּד)
**When to use nikud:**
- Children's books (ages 2-8): Full nikud required
- Poetry: Often includes nikud for clarity
- Religious texts: Traditional nikud patterns
- Educational materials: Partial or full nikud
- Adult fiction/non-fiction: Typically no nikud

**Nikud marks:**
- Kamatz (קָמָץ): a/o sound
- Patach (פַּתַח): a sound
- Tzere (צֵרֵי): e sound
- Segol (סֶגוֹל): e sound
- Hirik (חִירִיק): i sound
- Holam (חֹלָם): o sound
- Kubutz (קֻבּוּץ): u sound
- Shuruk (שׁוּרוּק): u sound (with vav)
- Shva (שְׁוָא): silent or short e

### Common Spelling Issues

**Yud (י) vs. Double Yud (יי):**
- עיר (city) - single yud
- עייר (tired) - double yud
- בין (between) - single yud
- בניין (building) - double yud

**Vav (ו) vs. Double Vav (וו):**
- חוק (law) - single vav
- חווק (pale) - double vav
- קו (line) - single vav
- קוו (hoped) - double vav

**Plene vs. Defective Spelling (כתיב מלא/חסר):**
- Modern Hebrew prefers plene (full) spelling
- Historical texts use defective (lacking vav/yud)
- Examples:
  - דוד vs. דד (uncle)
  - שומע vs. שמע (hears)

**Foreign Words:**
- Use phonetic Hebrew spelling
- Follow Academy of Hebrew Language guidelines
- Examples: קומפיוטר (computer), אינטרנט (internet)

## Hebrew Punctuation

### Primary Punctuation Marks

**Sof Pasuk (׃) - Similar to Period:**
- Used in biblical texts
- Modern Hebrew uses regular period (.)

**Comma (,)**
- Same as English usage
- Separates clauses and list items

**Question Mark (?)**
- Standard question mark
- Placed at end of question (right side in RTL)

**Exclamation Mark (!)**
- Same as English usage

**Quotation Marks:**
- Hebrew uses special quotation marks: ״...״
- Opening: ״ (gershayim pointing inward)
- Closing: ״ (gershayim pointing inward)
- Example: אמר ״שלום״
- Alternative: "..." (Western style, increasingly common)

### Special Hebrew Marks

**Geresh (׳):**
- Indicates abbreviation of single word
- Example: ר׳ (Rabbi)
- Used for single Hebrew letter representing number
- Example: ג׳ (3)

**Gershayim (״):**
- Indicates acronym or abbreviation
- Placed before last letter
- Example: רמב״ם (Rambam), צה״ל (IDF)
- Used for Hebrew numerals (multiple letters)
- Example: ט״ו (15)

**Maqaf (־):**
- Hebrew hyphen/dash
- Connects words in compound terms
- Example: בית־ספר (school)
- Connects prefixes: ה-, ל-, מ-, ש-
- Example: האיש, לבית

## Grammar Rules

### Gender Agreement
- Nouns have gender (masculine/feminine)
- Adjectives must agree in gender and number
- Verbs conjugate for gender in past tense

**Examples:**
- ספר גדול (masculine: big book)
- ספרה גדולה (feminine: big book - rare form)
- הילד קרא (the boy read)
- הילדה קראה (the girl read)

### Number Agreement
- Singular, plural, dual (limited use)
- Regular plural patterns:
  - Masculine: add ים-
  - Feminine: replace ה- with ות-

### Construct State (סמיכות)
- Possessive construction
- First word (Nismakh) loses definiteness
- Second word (Somekh) can be definite
- Example: בית הספר (the school = house-of the-study)

### Definite Article
- Only one: ה- (ha-)
- Attached to beginning of word
- Example: ספר (book) → הספר (the book)

## Typography Guidelines

### Font Selection
**Recommended Hebrew Fonts for Books:**
- Frank Ruehl CLM - Traditional serif, excellent for print
- David CLM - Clean serif, very readable
- Alef - Modern sans-serif
- Noto Serif Hebrew - Google's high-quality serif
- Miriam Libre - Open-source serif

**Avoid:**
- Very narrow fonts (hard to read Hebrew)
- Overly decorative fonts for body text
- Fonts with poor Hebrew support

### Font Size
- Body text: 12-14pt (Hebrew often needs slightly larger than English)
- Headings: 18-24pt
- Captions: 10-11pt

### Line Height (Leading)
- 1.5-1.6 for body text (slightly more than English)
- Hebrew letters are generally taller, need more vertical space

### Letter Spacing
- Hebrew generally needs less tracking than English
- Avoid excessive letter spacing
- Some fonts need kerning adjustment for certain letter pairs

### Direction and Alignment
**Right-to-Left (RTL):**
- Hebrew reads right to left
- Text alignment: right-aligned by default
- Page numbering: right to left
- Book binding: right side (back cover on left)

**Mixed Hebrew-English:**
- Overall direction: RTL
- Embedded English: LTR within RTL context
- Use Unicode bidirectional marks if needed:
  - LRM (Left-to-Right Mark): U+200E
  - RLM (Right-to-Left Mark): U+200F

## Common Errors and Fixes

### Spelling Errors

**את vs. אתה:**
- את - direct object marker or "you" (feminine)
- אתה - "you" (masculine)

**של vs. שֶׁל:**
- של - "of" (without nikud in adult text)
- With nikud: always שֶׁל

**היה vs. הייה:**
- היה - "was" (masculine)
- הייתה - "was" (feminine)
- Not: הייה (common error)

**Omitting dagesh:**
- פרי (fruit) - needs dagesh in פּ when beginning word
- Without modern nikud: not visible but understood

### Punctuation Errors

**Incorrect quotation marks:**
- ❌ "שלום" (Western quotes)
- ✓ ״שלום״ (Hebrew quotes preferred)
- ✓ "שלום" (acceptable in modern usage)

**Missing maqaf:**
- ❌ בית ספר
- ✓ בית־ספר

**Incorrect acronym marking:**
- ❌ צהל (no gershayim)
- ✓ צה״ל (gershayim before last letter)

### Grammar Errors

**Gender disagreement:**
- ❌ הילדה קרא
- ✓ הילדה קראה

**Incorrect construct state:**
- ❌ הבית של הספר
- ✓ בית הספר (preferred)
- ✓ הבית של הספר (acceptable but less concise)

## Style Guidelines

### Formal vs. Informal

**Formal Hebrew:**
- Full words, no slang
- Complex sentence structures
- Biblical/literary expressions
- Example: הילד קרא את הספר (The child read the book)

**Informal Hebrew:**
- Contractions acceptable
- Conversational tone
- Modern slang
- Example: הילד קרא את הספר (same, but context and surrounding text differ)

### Modern vs. Biblical

**Modern Hebrew:**
- Simplified grammar
- Borrowed words from other languages
- Standard punctuation

**Biblical Hebrew:**
- Archaic verb forms
- Special punctuation (sof pasuk)
- Full nikud system

### Academic Hebrew
- Follows Academy of Hebrew Language rules
- Technical terminology
- Formal structure
- Citations in Hebrew format

## Numbers in Hebrew

### Hebrew Numerals (Gematria)
- א = 1, ב = 2, ג = 3, etc.
- Used in dates, chapters, lists
- Marked with geresh (׳) or gershayim (״)
- Special cases to avoid: 15 and 16
  - Not י״ה (looks like God's name)
  - Use ט״ו (9+6) instead

### Arabic Numerals
- Standard: 1, 2, 3, etc.
- Direction: LTR even in Hebrew text
- Used in modern texts for most numbers

### Writing Numbers
- Small numbers (1-10): often written out
- Large numbers: usually Arabic numerals
- Dates: Hebrew numerals in traditional context
- Page numbers: can be Hebrew or Arabic

## Special Considerations for Book Editing

### Chapter Headings
- Use proper Hebrew heading hierarchy
- Can include chapter numbers in Hebrew numerals
- Example: פרק א׳ (Chapter 1)

### Table of Contents
- Right-aligned
- RTL ordering
- Page numbers on right side

### Footnotes and Endnotes
- Number RTL (1, 2, 3 or א, ב, ג)
- Text of note also RTL

### Dialogue
- Use proper Hebrew quotation marks: ״...״
- Em-dash for dialogue attribution
- Maintain RTL flow

### Poetry and Verse
- Often includes nikud
- Special line breaks and spacing
- Right-aligned
- May use special indentation patterns
